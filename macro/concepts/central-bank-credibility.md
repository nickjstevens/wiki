# Central Bank Credibility

## Definition
Central bank credibility is the market's and public's belief that monetary authorities understand the regime, communicate honestly, and can deliver price stability without being captured by fiscal or political constraints.

## April 2026 source signal
Jonathan Newman's Mises Wire account of Federal Reserve Governor Christopher Waller's Auburn visit emphasizes the gap between polished public Fed communication and more candid private remarks. The article's Austrian critique is that the Fed's technocratic veneer weakens under direct questioning, especially around inflation, interest-rate control, and institutional accountability.

The companion debt note adds the fiscal side: if debt service is already large and rising, central-bank credibility is not just about rhetoric. It depends on whether policy can resist monetizing sovereign debt when fiscal pressure intensifies.

## Why it matters for macro regime analysis
Credibility is a regime variable. When it is high, markets may believe inflation can be contained through conventional rate policy. When it weakens, hard-money narratives become more plausible and the adjustment can migrate into gold, [[bitcoin]], commodities, and the dollar-liquidity channels tracked in [[stablecoins]].

## Austrian / hard-money lens
The Austrian reading is skeptical of central-bank fine-tuning. It treats credit expansion, artificial rate suppression, and crisis-response money creation as sources of malinvestment and later instability. That links central-bank credibility directly to [[sovereign-debt-and-fiscal-dominance]] rather than treating the Fed as an independent technocratic machine.
