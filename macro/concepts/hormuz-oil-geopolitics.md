# Hormuz Oil Geopolitics

## Definition
Hormuz oil geopolitics covers how conflict, tolls, sanctions, shipping risk, and payment rails around the Strait of Hormuz transmit into oil prices, inflation expectations, dollar liquidity, and hard-asset narratives.

## April 2026 source signal
A Mises Wire article on a reported Iranian toll on oil moving through the Persian Gulf frames the issue through Rothbardian tax-incidence theory. It argues the toll's burden is not automatically shifted forward to final buyers; instead, the burden depends on existing margins, supply conditions, and who owns the relevant oil rents.

The article's macro-relevant claim is that physical disruption and capital destruction can lift oil prices, while the toll itself mainly reallocates rents. It also flags payment in yuan, rial, or [[bitcoin]] as a potential blow to petrodollar dominance if such mechanisms persist.

## Why it matters for cycle analysis
Hormuz is a chokepoint where geopolitical stress can become inflationary and liquidity-relevant. An oil shock can tighten real incomes, pressure central banks, worsen fiscal balances, and accelerate the [[late-cycle-risk-cascade]]. If payment conventions shift away from the dollar, the event also intersects with [[stablecoins]] and broader dollar-liquidity analysis.

## Hard-money interpretation
For this wiki's hard-money lens, the important point is second order: war risk is not just a headline commodity catalyst. It can change settlement preferences, reserve behavior, fiscal priorities, and the relative attractiveness of non-sovereign or scarce monetary assets.
