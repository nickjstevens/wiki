# Bitcoin

## Overview
Across this first batch of sources, Bitcoin is framed in multiple ways at once: as a store of value, the leading crypto asset during tightening liquidity, a tool for financial freedom under authoritarianism, a protocol facing long-horizon quantum risk, and a proof-of-work system with possible strategic or national-security implications.

## Store-of-value and market role
ARK's 2025 report presents Bitcoin as increasingly dormant and savings-like, noting low velocity and rising long-term holding behavior in 2024. Cowen's March 2026 report treats Bitcoin as the strongest part of crypto market structure, with altcoin underperformance acting as an early warning sign of tightening liquidity.

## Freedom and censorship-resistance role
The HRF annual report frames Bitcoin as practical infrastructure for dissidents, NGOs, and individuals facing surveillance, confiscation, demonetization, banking exclusion, and cross-border payment friction.

## Security and upgrade pressure
The ARK/Unchained paper treats quantum risk as real but gradual, while HRF presents a more urgent tone around the need to preserve Bitcoin's role as freedom-preserving infrastructure. This makes [[bitcoin-quantum-risk]] one of the first explicit contradiction-handling topics in the wiki.

## Competing interpretive frames
The sources do not reduce Bitcoin to a single narrative. Instead they suggest parallel lenses:
- savings asset and monetary network
- crypto market bellwether
- civil-liberties tool
- proof-of-work security system

## Geopolitical payment-rail signal
The April 2026 Hormuz toll article reports a claim that oil traffic out of the Persian Gulf could face a levy payable in bitcoin, yuan, or rial. Treat this as a time-sensitive geopolitical signal rather than a settled structural change: if such payment conventions persisted, Bitcoin would appear not only as a savings asset but also as a politically charged settlement rail in conflict-driven commodity trade. This links Bitcoin to [[hormuz-oil-geopolitics]], [[stablecoins]], and dollar-liquidity questions.
