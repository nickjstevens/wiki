# Base isolation with friction pendulum systems

Friction pendulum isolation shifts structural demand by lengthening period and introducing friction-based energy dissipation. The ingested paper highlights the sensitivity of isolated systems to near-fault ground-motion characteristics.

## Why it matters

- Isolation can materially reduce superstructure demand, but only if the isolation mechanism is modeled consistently with expected input motions.
- Near-fault pulses and displacement demands can challenge simplified assumptions.
- This topic ties back to [[nonstationary-ground-motion-generation-and-spectral-matching]] and [[nonlinear-seismic-assessment-methods]].

## Evidence from this ingest batch

- Petti et al. explicitly study friction pendulum isolated structures under near-fault events, emphasizing modeling and analysis rather than only conceptual advantages.

## Engineering takeaway

Isolation is not a universal demand eraser. Its benefit depends on the compatibility between isolation properties, displacement capacity, and the actual character of the hazard, especially when near-fault effects are important.
